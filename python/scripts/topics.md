### Chemistry
**Definition:** The field of study concerned with the characteristics, composition, and transformations of matter.

## Matter
**Definition:** Anything that has mass and occupies space.
*What isn't matter?*: Energy

# Characteristics
*What is a phase?*: One of three: solid, liquid or gas.
*What is a solid?*: Matter which has definite space and shape. Particles are only vibrating.
- Definite: Finite and unchanging.
- Example: Ice
*What is a liquid?*: Matter which has a definite space and indefinite shape. Particles move beside eachother.
- Indefinite: Ever changing,
- Example: Liquid water
*What is a gas?*: Matter which has indefinite shape and indefinite volume. Particles have negligible interactions. Compressable.
- Compressable: Able to be concentrated to a smaller volume.
- Volume: The region of space occupied. Measurable.
- Example: Steam
*What determines the phase of a material?*: Temperature, pressure and strength of forces holding particles together.
*What are chemical and physical properties?*: Distinguishing characteristics of a substance that is used in its identification and description. Unique to each substance.
- Physical: Relating to features of a substance which do not involve changing the identity of a substance.
- Example: Hardness, density, color, shape, malleability
- Chemical: Relating to features of a substance which involve its propensity or resistance to change in identity.
- Example: Rust, combustion, inert

# Transformations
*What are chemical and physical transformations?*: Changes of phase (physical) or identity (chemical).
# Pure vs. Mixture
*What is a pure substance?*: A sample of matter composed of only the same type of substance/chemical.
- Example: Distilled water
*What is a mixture?*: A homogenous or heterogenous sample of matter composed of multiple substances/chemicals.
- Homogenous: A uniform phase with equivalent distribution across the whole sample which is inseparable by physical means.
  - Example: Seawater
- Heterogenous: A multi-phase material with inequivalent distribution across the whole sample which is separable by physical means.
  - Example: Oil and water

# Elements and Compounds
*What is an element?*: An element is a pure substance composed of one one type of atom, which has the same chemical and physical properties. A element cannot be chemically broken down.
*What is a compound?*: A compound is a pure substance composed of multiple types of atoms, forming a new material with distinct chemical and physical properties from its components. A compound can be broken down into subcomponents.
*What is the periodic table (brief)?*: The periodic table is a chemist's tool for organizing elements by their chemical and physical properties.

*What is a chemical symbol?*: A one to two letter shorthand starting with a capital and ending with lowercase, representative of one element.

# Atoms and Molecules
*What is an atom?*: The smallest fundamental particle of an element which can exist and still have the same properties of the element.
*What is a molecule (brief)?*: A unit composed of atoms described by its presence of one or more elements (homoatomic, heteroatomic).

## Measurements in Chemistry
**Definition:** The determiniation of the dimensions, capacity, quantity or extent of something.
- Example: Temperature, volume, length, time, mass

# Measurment Systems
*What is a measurement system?*: A set of agreed upon numbers associated with chemical and physical phenomena. 2 (SI and English).
*What is a unit?*: Units are terms to describe in respect to one another the magnitude of a measurment.
*What are SI units?*: A standard system related by base 10 (log<sub>10</sub>(x) or 10<sup>x</sup>.
- Examles: centimeter, kilometer, gram, milliliter, liter
*What are Enlgish units?*: A common system unrelated by a common factor.
- Examples: inch, mile, foot, pound, ounce.
*What is a base unit?*: Base units are reference points in the SI system which are effectively 10<sup>0</sup> (1 to 1 ratio).
*What is the connection between English, SI or intersystem units?*: Conversion factors are derived by definitions and used to convert between units.
- Example: 1 yard = 3 feet, 1000 meters = 1 kilometer

# Uncertainty
*What is uncertainty?*: Every measurement device has a limited degree of precision, and the observer has a degree of error.
*How to calculate uncertainty?*: The digit after the last digit observable by a physical device.
- Example: A meter stick has increments of centimeters, and the last digit by 5/10th if too small to determine 1/10th, otherwise 1/10th.
 - Visible value: 1.003. Estimated digit: 1.003(0) or 1.003(5).
- Example: For an electronic device the degree of precision is the last digit.
  - Visible value: 1.0030. Estimated digit: 1.003(0)

# Significant Figures
*What is a significant figure?*: Significant figures is a count of the number of relevant digits to a measurement for a given value.
*What is an exact number?*: An exact number is a number with infinite significant figures, and is determined by counting or by a definition.
- Example: 12 donuts = 1 dozen, 1000 meters = 1 kilometer
*What is an inexact number?*: An inexact number is a calculated value with a string number of significant figures, determined by observation of a measurement device.
- Example: 1.0003 centimeters
*What is accuracy?*: Accuracy is proximity to the correct value.
- Example: 1.003, 1.000, 1.004 is accurate to the number 1.
*What is precision?*: Precision is the degree of specificity of a number, normally representative of many significant figures.
- Example: 0.00000001 is more precise than 0.01
*How does one calculate significant figures?*:
1. All zeroes to the left of a non-zero digit are insignificant.
2. All non-zero digits are significant.
3. All zeroes between two non-zero digits are significant.
4. All trailing zeroes are significant.

# Mathematical Operations with Significant Figures
*How does one determine the number of correct significant figures of a mathematical operation?*:
- Multiplication / Division
  - The number with the least amount of significant figures restricts the calculated value to be that many significant figures. Round the last digit.
  - Example: 5.03 * 5.0 = 25.2
- Addition / Subtraction
  - The number with the lowest precision determines the number of decimal places of the calculated value. Round the last digit.
  - Example: 1.00 - 2 = -1

# Scientific Notation
*What is scientific notation?*: Scientific notation is leveraging base 10 to represent a number in the range 1 < 10 multiplied by 10 to the nth.
- Example: 10004 = 1.0004 x 10<sup>4</sup>

# Dimensional Analysis
*What is dimensional analysis?*: Dimensional analysis is a mathematical problem solving skill where measurments are converted an equivalent value with significant figures in mind.
- Example: How fast did you drive to school in kilometers/minute?
  1. English units are miles / hour.
  2. Determine the conversion of miles to meters, and meters to kilometers.
  3. Determine the conversion of minutes to hours.
  4. Perform mathematical operations, either dividing or multiplying by a correct fraction (if cancelling a unit, the unit must divide the original).
  5. Represent the final answer in correct significant figures.
- Example: How to solve mass by displacement and density?
  1. Determine the density of a substance.
  2. Put the sample in a measured volume of a liquid.
  3. Record the new volume.
  4. Calculate the difference of volume.
  5. Apply mass / volume = density and algebraically solve.

## The Periodic Table and the Atom
# Internal composition of an Atom
*What composes an atom?*: Atoms are made of three subatomic particles: the electron, the proton and the neutron.
- Electron: Negatively charged, occupies the majority of the atom's volume. Very low mass compared to the proton and neutron.
- Proton: Positively charged, occupying the nucleus of the atom.
- Neutron: Uncharged, also occupying the nucleus of the atom.

# Atomic Number, Mass Number and the Complete Chemical Symbol
*What is the atomic number of an atom?*: The number of protons.
*What is the mass number of an atom?*: The summation of protons and neutrons.
*What is the complete chemical symbol of an atom?*:
  1. The mass number in the top left.
  2. The atomic number in the bottom left.
  3. The chemical symbol in the center.
*What is the relationship of the atomic number and the periodic table?*:
- Elements are categorized in order of atomic number.

# Isotopes and Atomic Masses
*What is an isotope?*: An atom with a definite number of neutrons.
*What is atomic mass?*: A calculated value by considering the abundance of an element's naturally occurying isotopes, determined by an average of their atomic masses.

# Periodic Law
*What is periodic law?: Elements have predictable physical and chemical properties. Elements in columns of the periodic table have similar chemical and physical properties. Elements in rows of the periodic table have similar distance of electrons from their nucleus.
*What are the representative elements?*: Elements with predictable and well known properties,
*What are the alkali metals?*: Group I
*What are the alkaline earth metals?*: Group II
*What are the transition elements?*: Group BI through BX
*What are the nonmetals?*: Hydrogen and elements commonly found as gases or in molecular compounds.
*What are the halogens?*: Group XVII
*What are the noble gases?*: Group XVIII
*What are the inner transition elements?*: Lathinides and Actinides

# Metals and Nonmetals
*What are metals?*: Elements with luster, malleability, high conductivity, high density and high melting points.
*What are nonmetals?*: Elements lacking luster, brittle, nonconductive, low density and low melting points.
*Where on the periodic table defines the line between nonmetals and metals?*: A staircase beginning between boron and aluminum down to the right.

# Electron Arrangements
*What is an electron shell?*: The quantized energy level where electrons reside where increasing number correlates to size from the nucleus.
*What is an electron subshell?*: s, p, d, f. Each subshell occupies with different shapes of volume.
*What is an electron orbital?*: A region of space within an electron subshell which can be occupied by a maximum of two electrons with equal and opposite spin.
*Are electron orbitals of different energy?*: They are degenerate and are filled first by one electron in each, before pairing up.
*Do electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first?*: Yes, they must occupy the lowest energy orbitals first.
*What is an electron configuration?*: Two forms, long-form and noble gas-form. Each subshell on each energy level is represented by first the shell number, followed by the subshell letter and the number of electrons filling that subshell in a superscript.
- Example: Neon = 1s<sup>2</sup>2s<sup>2</sup>2p<sup>6</sup> = [He]2s<sup>2</sup>2p<sup>6</sup>
*What is an electron orbital diagram?*: A diagram with a y-axis of energy showing a box for each orbital, at the same vertical height, filled with electron pairs up till its electron count.
*What is the distinguishing electron?*: The electron of an atom with the highest energy.

## Chemical Bonds
**Definition:** The sharing or exchange of electrons, forming a tight couple between two or more atoms.

## Ionic Compounds
*What is an ionic compound?*: An ionic compound is determined by a compound with an ionic bond.
- Characteristics
  - High melting point
  - High electrical conductivity as a liquid or in solution
*What is an ionic bond?*: An ionic bond is the donation of an electron between of one atom to another, forming a bond.

# Valence Electrons
*What are valence electrons? (bried)*: Valence electrons are the last 8 electrons of an atom.
*What is a lewis structure?*: An organization of valence electrons around and between atoms.

# Ions
*What is an ion?*: An ion is an atom which has lost or gained electron(s).
*Why do some atoms lose electrons and others gain electrons?*: Per the octet rule, the lowest energy transformation is preferred and atoms will gain or lose as little electrons as possible to return to a state with a full outer electron shell.
*How many valence electrons and which charge does alkali metals adopt?*: 1 and +1
*How many valence electrons and which charge does alkaline earth metals adopt?*: 2 and +2
*How many valence electrons and which charge does halogens adopt?*: 7 and -1
*How many valence electrons and which charge does chalcogens adopt?*: 6 and -2
*How many valence electrons and which charge does pnictogens adopt?*: 5 and =3
*How many valence electrons do fixed charge metals adopt?*:
  - Ag +1
  - Cd +2
  - Zn +2
  - Ga +3
  - Al +3
*What charge do other metals adopt?*: Varies.
*What interactions are present in ions?*: Ionic compounds arrange which maximizies attractiions between ions of opposite charge. Arrangements are usually symmetrical with regular shapea ans definite angles.

# Lewis Structures
*How does one draw a lewis structure?*: Charged ions are encapsulated in brackets with their ion's valence electron count around all 4 sides, 1 electron per side and paired after all four have one occupying. The charge is a superscript on the top right with a number in front of the charge except for the number 1.

# Chemical Formulae from Ions
*How is a formula determined from ions?*: The lowest multiples of each atom is designated with a subscript, forming a neutral compound from the charges cancelling out.
*What is a formula unit?*: Ionic compounds are lattices of atoms, but have a consistent ratio of one atom to another.

# Binary Compounds
*What is a binary compound?*: A binary compound is a ionic formula unit containing only two types of atoms, between a metal and a nonmetal.

# Nomenclature
*How is a binary ionic compound with a metal of fixed charge named?*:
  1. The full name of the metal is named first.
  2. The nonmetal is named with the suffix -ide.
*How is a binary ionic compound with a metal of a variable charge named?*:
  1. The full name of the metal is named first.
  2. The metal is followed by a roman numeral in parentheses representing its charge.
  3. The nonmetal is named with the suffix -ide.

# Polyatomics
*What is a polyatomic ion?*: A heteroatomic ion between two nonmetals with a fixed overall charge.
*What are the common polyatomic ions?*:
  1. Nitrate
  2. Ammonium
  3. Sulfate
  4. Phosphate
  5. Carbonate
  6. Bicarbonate
  7. Acetate
  8. Cyanide
  9. Perchlorate
  10. Chlorate
  11: Hydroxide
  12. Dichromate
*How are polyatomic ionic compounds named?*:
  1. Ammonium and then the nonmetal followed by -ide or a negatively charged polyatomic.
  1. Metals are named similarly to previous nomenclature, followed by a polyatomic.
*How do ionic formulas look for polyatomics?*: Similar to ionic compounds, with the caveat of adding parenthesis around the polyatomic formula if more than 1 are present.

## Covalent Molecules
**Definition:** Compounds with covalent bonding (equal sharing) of electrons between two or more non-metals.
**What is the difference between molecular compounds and ions?*: Molecular compounds are usually liquid, or gas at room temperature with low boiling points.

# Covalent Bonding
**How does the octet rule apply to covalent compounds?*: The electrons shared between two atoms in a bond count toward their noble gas configuration.
- Example Fluorine and Fluorine have 7 electrons each, they share one from each, having two in between them, the two counts toward each noble gas configuration (6+2) and (6+2) making 8.
**What is the shorthand for drawing bonds?*: A line between two atoms shows one bond, with extra lines representing double and triple bonds respectively.
**What is a nonbonding electron?*: Electrons which do not participate in the bonding between atoms in a molecule, shown deliberately in their Lewis structure.
**What inferences can be made to determine the lewis structure of a molecule?*: Atoms will still like to achieve the same number of shared electrons as they would with electron exchange in ionic compounds.

# Coordinate Covalent Bonding
**What is a coordinate covalent bond?*: A covalent bond (shared electron pair) where both electrons come from the same atom.
- Example: HClO<sub>2</sub> H-O-Cl-O, both electrons come from chlorine to oxygen.

# Drawing Lewis Structures
**How does one draw lewis structures?**:
  1. Normally the central atom is the atom with the lowest count.
  2. Draw each atom around the central atom with its valence electrons.
  3. Draw lines between each single electron to the central atom.
  4. Move the non-bonding electron pair from one of the outer atoms on to the bond if the central atom lacks an octet until it has an octet.
  5. Count the electrons around each atom and if it equals 8 and matches the number of valence electrons then it is correct.
  6. Splitting each bond in half, if the atom contains more or less electrons than its valence count, calculate the charge.
    - Example: HClO<sub>2</sub> has 8 electrons around chlorine, with two bonds. Splitting each bond leaves chlorine with 6, which is one less than its original 7, inducing a + charge. The oxygen on the edge started with 6, but splitting its bond leaves 7, which is one more than its original 6, leaving a - charge. The positive and negative charge leave the molecule uncharged.
    - Example: Ammonium has a positive charge because nitrogen after splitting the four bonds leaves 4 on nitrogen, which is one less than its original 5.
  7. Only form coordinate covalent bonds if no other possible configuration is possible.

# Molecular Geometry
**What is an electron group?**: An electron group is a single, double, triple bond or lone pair.
**What is a molecule's molecular geometry**
  1. Count the number of electron groups around the central atom.
  2. Determine how many are bonding and how many are non-bonding.
  3. 4 electron groups
    - 4 bonding 0 non-bonding = tetrahedral
    - 3 bonding 1 non-bonding = trigonal pyramidal
    - 2 bonding 2 non-bonding = bent/angular
  4. 3 electron groups
    - 3 bonding 0 non-bonding = trigonal planar
    - 2 bonding 1 non bonding = bent/angular
  5. 2 electron groups
    - 2 bonding 0 non-bonding = linear
**What is a molecule's electron geometry?**
  - 4 electron groups = tetrahedral
  - 3 electron groups = trigonal planar
  - 2 electron groups = linear

# Electronegativity
**What is electronegativity?**: The propensity of an atom to bear an electron charge on a scale of 0 - 4 with 4 being the greatest.

# Bond Polarity
**What is polarity?**: Polarity is a function of a difference in electronegativity between two atoms.
**When is a bond polar?**: When there is a difference of electrongativity betwen 0.4-2.0 if the atoms are non-metals.
**When is a bond nonpolar?**: When they are the same atom of have a difference of electronegativity less than 0.4.
**When is a bond ionic?**: When two atoms have a electronegativity greater than 1.5 if between a metal and a nonmetal.

# Molecular Polarity
**When is a molecule polar?**: When there are polar bonds which do not cancel eachother out.
  - Example: Water has polar bonds, but its molecular geometry is bent and therefore the dipoles do not pull directly on eachother but rather at angles.
**When is a molecule nonpolar?**: When there are only non-polar bonds or when two polar bonds cancel eachother out
  - Example: C<sub>2<\sub>H<sub>6</sub> only has non-polar bonds (C-H and C-C)
  - Example: CO<sub>2<\sub> has polar bonds but the molecular geometry is linear and therefore cancel eachother out.

# Binary Molecular Compound Naming
**How are binary molecular compounds named?**:
  1. The first appearing atom is normally partially positive (lower electronegativity) and is named with a prefix denoting its count unless it has a count of 1.
  2. The second appearing atom is normally partially negative (higher electronegativity) and is named with a prefix including mono- denoting its count.
